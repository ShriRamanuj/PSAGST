Ch.1 — Self-Assessment Regime | Burden of Proof | Evasion vs Non-Payment ⇧ Index
⚖️ Chapter 1 — Introduction | Foundational Principles for Taxpayer Protection
1.4 Self-Assessment Tax Regime
GST = SELF-ASSESSMENT
Taxpayer files returns and self-determines liability
Revenue must show prima facie case exists against taxpayer's determination
📝 In GST, you determine your own tax. Revenue cannot simply disagree and leave you to prove you are right. Revenue must first show WHY your self-assessment is wrong — with evidence, not with suspicion.
PROHIBITED
It does NOT suffice to put forward an alternate interpretation and leave taxpayer to prove correctness of self-assessment
Violative of §59 CGST Act
1.5 No Burden to Prove Innocence
Taxpayer is NOT required to prove innocence
State must first discharge its burden of proving evasion
Presumption of regularity in taxpayer's affairs unless shown otherwise
📝 "Prove your innocence" is a criminal law trap that the Revenue sometimes sets. In GST, you don't have to prove you are innocent. Revenue must prove you are guilty — first. Until then, your self-assessment stands.
1.6 Burden in Tax Evasion
BURDEN vs ONUS
Burden = always on the party the law states
Onus = shifts when new material introduced
Revenue must make out prima facie case first → then burden shifts to taxpayer to rebut
📝 "Burden" is fixed by law — Revenue must establish evasion. "Onus" is different — it shifts when evidence is introduced. Once Revenue produces evidence of evasion, taxpayer must rebut it. But Revenue must go first.
Narayan Bhagwantrao Gosavi Balajiwale v. Gopal Vinayak Gosavi AIR 1960 SC 100:
When both sides introduce evidence, question of 'burden of proof' becomes academic
💡 Care must be taken NOT to displace the burden by doing anything that causes it to shift onto the taxpayer — e.g., responding on merits without first objecting to jurisdiction allows the narrative to shift. Always raise jurisdictional objections first.
Ch.1 — Evidence Act | Administrative Law | Natural Justice | §160(1) Limitations ⇧ Index
📋 Procedural Protections — Evidence Standards | Natural Justice | Defective Notice = Fatal
1.7 Indian Evidence Act Principles Apply
Chuharmal v. UoI AIR 1988 SC 1384:
Equitable principles of common law in Indian Evidence Act → applicability to taxation matters not barred → must be admitted to establish facts
📝 Revenue cannot make loose allegations in a notice. The standard of establishing facts must meet the evidentiary standards of the Indian Evidence Act — even though GST is not a court proceeding. Statements must be made responsibly, not speculatively.
1.8 Administrative Law Standards
Administrative action must meet standards of 'equity, justice and good conscience'
Officer who conducts audit/investigation CANNOT adjudicate = violation of Nemo Judex Rule
Circular 31/5/2018-GST (amended by 169/1/2022-GST) mandates transfer of file to Executive Commissionerate for adjudication
📝 The investigating officer and the adjudicating officer MUST be different persons. This is the fundamental principle of natural justice — no one can be a judge in their own cause. CBIC Circular enforces this separation for central tax.
EP Royappa v. State of TN AIR 1974 SC 555:
Passion to protect interests of Revenue does NOT authorize bypassing 'due process' laid down in law
Defective SCN = Fatal
CCE v. Brindavan Beverages (P) Ltd. (2007) 213 ELT 487 (SC):
"Defective notice is incurable and fatal to the demand"
§75(7) CGST: Grounds on which ORDER may be passed must be the VERY GROUNDS on which NOTICE was issued
No adjudicating authority empowered to cure deficiencies in notice
📝 The adjudicating officer cannot "fix" a defective SCN by giving better reasons in the order. The order must stand on the same grounds as the notice. If the notice is vague or wrong — the order falls too.
§160(1) CGST — Saves Only If:
'Substance and effect' of communication conveys its 'intent, purpose and requirements'
Mere reference to wrong section can be saved if substance is clear
💡 "Show cause why penalty as per law should not be demanded" — §160(1) CANNOT save this (too vague). BUT "show cause why penalty under Section 122 should not be demanded" — this can be saved because substance of allegation is clear, even if specific sub-section not stated.
1.9 Audi Alteram Partem
State of Orissa v. Dr (Ms.) Binapani Dey AIR 1967 SC 1269:
"Even where statute does not require specifically that principles of natural justice are to be followed, they MUST be followed because when any proceeding is capable of adversely affecting rights of any person, adherence is imperative"
📝 The principles of natural justice apply even when the law doesn't explicitly say so. ANY proceeding that can hurt someone's rights must follow natural justice — no exceptions.
Notice cannot expect noticee to 'fill in the blanks'
Must be put at notice clearly, completely, comprehensively with evidence
Secret allegations incapacitate noticee's defence
1.18 Three Principles of Natural Justice in GST
① Nemo Judex In Causa Sua
No one should be judge in their own cause
Investigating officer ≠ Adjudicating officer
② Audi Alteram Partem
Hear the other side
Clear allegations + evidence + opportunity to respond
③ Speaking Order
Decision must be reasoned
Grounds in order = grounds in notice (§75(7))
Ch.1 — Active Disobedience | Arrest of Consultant | Confession vs Admission | Intention vs Motive ⇧ Index
🛡️ Taxpayer's Rights in Invalid Proceedings | Key Distinctions
1.10 Active Disobedience NOT Fault When Proceedings Invalid
RULE
Where proceeding is invalid and does not explicitly disclose law under which initiated → taxpayer CANNOT legally be found at fault for disobedience
📝 If the notice/inspection/summons is illegal — refusing to comply is NOT contempt. You are not disobeying the law — you are disobeying an illegal action. But you must "politely put at notice" the Proper Officer of the illegality before approaching High Court.
Vigilantibus non dormientibus jura subveniunt
"Law will not help those who sleep on their rights"
Must actively contest abuse — politely 'put at notice' the Proper Officer
📝 Don't ignore illegal proceedings — contest them. Don't rush to High Court — first write to the officer explaining the illegality. Often, officers will correct themselves when the illegality is pointed out politely.
1.11 Limited Scope for Arrest of CA/Consultant
§122 STRUCTURE
§122(1) → 'Taxable persons'
§122(1A) → 'Mastermind' of fictitious billing
§122(2) → 'Registered persons'
§122(3) → 'Any person' (max ₹25,000 CGST penalty)
📝 A CA/consultant can only be penalised under §122(3) at most — ₹25,000 under CGST (plus ₹25,000 under SGST). UNLESS the CA is shown to be the "mastermind" of the fictitious billing (§122(1A)) — then serious action can follow.
§132(1)
"Whoever commits" OR "whoever causes to commit and retains benefits"
'Commit' = the MIND, not the HAND
Hand = accessory only
Arrest of consultant only if shown to be principal offender (mind)
📝 §132 distinguishes between the mind (the one who planned/authorized the offence) and the hand (the one who executed it). A CA who prepared returns is the "hand." Unless the CA is shown to be the "mind" behind the evasion — arrest under §69 is "wholly impermissible."
1.13 Confession vs Admission
Confession
Acknowledges GUILT of the offence
Inculpatory in nature
Admission of ALL facts constituting offence
Admission
Acknowledges a FACT only
Not necessarily inculpatory
May be exculpatory or neutral
📝 During an inspection/summons, be careful what you say. Admitting a factual mistake (e.g., "yes, we filed the return late") is an ADMISSION — not a confession of tax evasion. Do not let Revenue convert an admission of fact into a confession of guilt.
💡 "I acknowledge that GSTR-3B and GSTR-2A differ for the period" = Admission of a discrepancy (factual). This is NOT the same as confessing "I wrongfully availed ITC." Revenue must still prove the ITC was wrongfully availed — the admission of a discrepancy does not do that work.
1.14 Intention vs Motive
Intention
What the person meant to do with the act
Relevant to determine offence
Governs §74 (fraud/suppression = intentional)
Motive
Why the person wanted the act done
NOT relevant to guilt
Good motive does NOT excuse an illegal act; Bad motive does NOT convict an otherwise legal act
📝 "I didn't intend to evade tax — I just made a mistake" = Intention defence (relevant, can succeed). "I evaded tax but my motive was to save the business" = Motive defence (irrelevant — motive doesn't matter once the act is done with intention).
1.15 ACCIDENT vs MISTAKE
Accident = unforeseeable event causing unintended result
Mistake = foreseeable event causing unintended result due to human error
Both negate criminal intention → important for §132 prosecution
Ch.1 — Spot Recovery | Rule of Law | Forced Payment in GST ⇧ Index
🚫 Spot Recovery = Illegal | Rule of Law Stands Tall in GST
1.16 Forced / Spot Recovery
Forced recovery or 'spot recovery' in GST = ILLEGAL
Any payment made under compulsion/coercion during inspection = NOT voluntary
May be claimed as refund under 'others' category (§54)
📝 During inspection, officers sometimes pressure taxpayers to "pay immediately" via DRC-03 to avoid arrest. This is illegal. Any payment made under such coercion is NOT voluntary. The taxpayer can immediately file for refund of such coerced payment. You cannot be compelled to pay without a proper adjudication process.
💡 If forced to pay ₹50 lakhs during inspection via DRC-03 to "avoid arrest" — immediately file refund application under §54 under "others" category. Circular No. 125/44/2019-GST dated 18 Nov 2019 requires Proper Officer to issue acknowledgement under Rule 90 if application is prima facie free from defects. Refund of such amount does not bear any reference to investigation proceedings.
1.17 Rule of Law Stands Tall
GST is governed by Rule of Law
Passion to protect Revenue interest does NOT authorize bypassing due process
Every action by Proper Officer must have statutory foundation
📝 "We are doing this for the country / revenue / national interest" is NOT a justification for bypassing the statutory procedure. Due process is the protection Parliament built into the law. Bypassing it is illegal regardless of the stated motive.
Intelligence must be gathered BEFORE invoking exceptional powers of inspection
Suspicion and doubt = insufficient
A 'fishing expedition' = demands active (polite) disobedience
💡 Letter G (in Handbook Appendix): "Request to Refrain from Pursuing Taxpayer to Deposit Dues Without Issuing Valid Notice" — Politely inform Revenue that no admission of liability is being made, all authorizations (except for furnishing factual information) are rescinded, and further proceedings as per law may be pursued. This letter protects against coerced payments.
Ch.2 §67 — Authorization | Exhausted by Exercise | No Follow-up Correspondence | Intelligence Requirement ⇧ Index
🔐 Section 67 — Inspection of Premises | Authorization = Exceptional Power | Strict Requirements
📝 Section 67 is an EXCEPTIONAL power — not a routine audit. It requires pre-existing intelligence of tax evasion before the authorization is issued. The authorization is tightly bound — specific officer, specific place, specific time. Once exercised, it is exhausted. No fishing expeditions allowed.
2.1 Precondition for §67 Authorization
TRIGGER
Authorized Officer must have 'reasons to believe' that:
(i) Taxable person has suppressed goods liable to seizure
OR (ii) Books/accounts are kept which are likely to cause evasion
📝 "Reasons to believe" ≠ "reason to suspect." Belief must be based on tangible intelligence, not mere suspicion. The file notings recording reasons to believe MUST exist at the time of issuing the authorization — not created afterwards.
§2 IMPORTANT
Inspection §67 → covers 'evasion of tax' matters ONLY
Access to business premises (§71) → does NOT authorize inspection under §67
Routine audit issues CANNOT be raised in §67 proceedings
2.9 Authorization — Exhausted by Exercise
Authorization in Form GST INS-01 is specific to:
(i) Specific officer — no sub-delegation
(ii) Specified places — precise, not vague
(iii) Within specified time (day and date stated)
EXHAUSTION RULE
Once Authorized Officer executes authorization and EXITS premises → authorization expires
ONE authorization = ONE visit
Multiple visits require multiple Form GST INS-01s
📝 If officer leaves and comes back the next day — they need a fresh authorization. The original INS-01 is dead the moment they exit the premises after the first visit. This is a powerful safeguard against indefinite harassment.
2.10 No Follow-up Correspondence
Once INS-01 exhausted by exercise → NO provision in law to issue 'endorsement' or 'letters' calling for explanation
Not by correspondence, not in-person
NO "follow-up correspondence" is authorized under §67
📝 Any letter received from the investigation wing after inspection saying "please explain / clarify / produce documents" — there is NO legal basis for this. You may reply or not reply. If you reply, it is open to retraction at adjudication stage. Only summons under §70 can be used — but only after careful consideration.
💡 Letter B in Handbook Appendix: "Request to Refrain from Calling for Information Not Authorized under §67" — Politely challenge non-descript letters asking for explanations by pointing out no such power exists after INS-01 is exhausted.
2.27 Intelligence Needed BEFORE Invoking
Intelligence about 'evasion of tax' CANNOT be collected from taxpayer by issuing summons or non-descript letters
Intelligence must be gathered BEFORE invoking exceptional powers — not after
Lack of intelligence = misapplication = fishing expedition
📝 Revenue cannot use summons to build up a case from nothing. They must have intelligence FIRST, then authorize inspection. Using inspection/summons to go fishing for problems = abuse of power.
ROUTINE MATTERS ≠ §67
ITC mismatch (GSTR-2A vs 3B) / §16(1) / §16(4) / §17(2) / §17(5) / Rules 40-44 / Interest on gross tax / RCM / Staff recoveries / Other income / Discounts & subsidy — these are NOT evasion matters for §67 purposes
📝 These routine matters must be raised in §65 audit or §73/74 SCN proceedings — NOT in §67 inspection. An SCN issued under §74 (fraud) for ITC mismatch without evidence of fraud = misapplication of §67 powers. This is the basis for challenging jurisdiction of the entire proceeding.
Ch.2 §67 — 'Secreted' Articles | Seizure to 'Secure & Identify' | What Cannot Be Seized ⇧ Index
🗄️ What Can Be Seized | 'Secreted' — The Critical Threshold
📝 Seizure under §67(2) is ONLY available for "secreted" articles. If goods are openly displayed, properly accounted, and visible — they are NOT secreted. Seizure of non-secreted articles is illegal and can be challenged. Seizure does NOT transfer ownership — it is just securing and identifying for later proceedings.
2.7 What Is 'Secreted'?
Articles coupled with use of some 'device' that makes detection IMPOSSIBLE
Includes: undisclosed location, misleading documentation, passwords/cloud storage/ghost files, unaccounted stocks
Openly kept but in UNDISCLOSED location = secreted
Non-accounting of goods = secreted (concealment by omission to document)
📝 "Secreted" is not just physically hidden. It includes: goods stored openly but at an unregistered/undisclosed location, goods not entered in any books, invoices deliberately misdescribed. But goods that are properly documented and visible in normal course of business = NOT secreted.
2.8 Non-Secreted = Cannot Be Seized
Articles NOT secreted → revenue limited to tax + interest + penalty under §122
Confiscation requires: (i) secreted AND (ii) detected in search proceedings
Additional places of business not added to registration but operating openly with GSTIN displayed = prima facie NOT secreted → NO seizure, NO confiscation
📝 Unregistered additional places of business are a compliance violation — but NOT necessarily "secreted." If goods are kept openly without any concealment device — §67(2) seizure cannot apply. Revenue can only demand tax, interest, and penalty under §122.
2.11 Seizure = 'Secure and Identify' Only
Seizure = take physical custody to 'secure' and 'identify' for later proceedings
Does NOT imply transfer of property in goods (that would be confiscation)
Form GST INS-02 must be drawn up + two witnesses (panchas) to attest
📝 Seized goods don't become Revenue's property. Revenue is just "holding" them securely to use as evidence. The taxpayer still owns the goods. After seizure, goods may be handed back for safekeeping — the INS-02 is the formal record of what was seized.
2.12 Seizure NOT to PREVENT ACCESS by taxpayer
Taxpayer retains right to inspect/access seized goods and documents
§67(5): taxpayer may apply to Commissioner in terms of §165(5) Cr.PC for copy of seized documents
Three-Category Framework
SECRETED ARTICLES
Goods and other articles secreted in place of search
SEIZED ARTICLES
Secreted articles comprising (i) goods liable to confiscation + (ii) documents, books, things — seized against INS-02
OFFENDING ARTICLES
Goods liable to confiscation — whether seized or NOT — including conveyance used in committing offence
Ch.2 §67 — Seizure of Cash | Electronic Records | Provisional Release | Prohibition Orders ⇧ Index
💰 Seizure Process | Special Assets | Provisional Release Rights
2.13 Seizure of 'Cash'
Cash is NOT goods liable to confiscation under any of the five clauses of §130(1)
Cash = NOT seizable under §67(2) as 'goods'
📝 Officers sometimes try to seize cash during inspection claiming it is "unaccounted money." This is legally impermissible under §67 — cash is not goods liable to confiscation. Cash seizure under GST law has a separate (very limited) basis — not a general power during inspection.
2.20: Seizure NOT a form of 'recovery'
Seizure cannot be used as a mechanism to recover taxes
Provisional release with or without bond + security = path to release of seized goods
2.22 Seizure of Electronic Records
Electronic records = 'things' under §67(2)
Can be seized if secreted (e.g., password-protected files, ghost folders, cloud storage)
Authorized Officer may take custody of device or copy of data
📝 Emails, cloud documents, password-protected files — if they contain evidence of tax evasion and were concealed — can be seized. But the seizure must follow Form GST INS-02 procedure with panchas. Officers cannot arbitrarily copy all data from your server.
2.17 Provisional Release
TAXPAYER RIGHT
Provisional release of seized goods available upon execution of bond + security
Taxpayer may seek provisional release at ANY stage before confiscation order
📝 You don't have to wait for adjudication to get your goods back. You can seek provisional release immediately after seizure — by furnishing a bond (undertaking to return goods if confiscation ordered) plus security (bank guarantee or cash).
2.18 Refusal of provisional release = NOT APPEALABLE
BUT the ORDER refusing release must still be a speaking/reasoned order
Subject to writ jurisdiction of High Court
💡 Provisional release refused = cannot file APL-01. But can file Writ Petition in High Court challenging the refusal — especially if the order refusing release is not reasoned. The order of refusal being non-appealable makes the writ remedy even more important.
2.16 Prohibition Orders | 2.19 Omission to Seize
PROHIBITION ORDER
Where Authorized Officer cannot seize goods (too large/immovable) → may order that goods NOT be removed from premises
Different from seizure — goods stay but cannot be moved
2.19 — OMISSION TO SEIZE
If inspection is completed WITHOUT seizure → inspection under §67 is COMPLETE
No further proceeding remains under §67
Investigation cannot continue indefinitely — §67(7) prescribes time limit
📝 If officers completed inspection and left without seizing anything — §67 is over. They cannot come back and say "we forgot to seize documents." The absence of seizure is an implicit acknowledgement that no secreted articles were found. This is a strong defensive point.
Ch.2 §67 — SCN After Inspection | What Can Be Demanded | §67(10) Application Right ⇧ Index
📋 SCN in §67 Proceedings — Scope | Limits | Taxpayer's §67(10) Right
2.23 SCN Must Touch 'Reasons to Believe'
Allegations in SCN must touch 'reasons to believe' contained in file noting for grant of authorization
Cannot travel so far away from these reasons as to be an 'after discovery'
Trespass of §67 into territory of §65 = illegal
📝 If inspection was authorized for "unaccounted goods" but SCN demands tax on "wrong classification of services" — there is a disconnect. The SCN allegation must be traceable to the original reasons to believe. An "after discovery" demand (finding something new during inspection) cannot become the basis of a §67 SCN.
SCN under §73 ipse dixit (without evasion) in §67 proceedings = misapplication of powers
Notice must be for evasion matters — not for routine ITC/valuation/classification disputes
2.25 Matters Arising from Inspection
After §67 proceedings concluded → matters may proceed as:
(i) Normal SCN under §73/74 (with or without extended time)
(ii) Confiscation under §130 (if offending articles)
(iii) Prosecution under §132 (if offences committed)
(iv) Referral to §65 audit (for non-evasion matters found)
2.26: Review of ROUTINE matters NOT in inspection
Routine matters discovered during inspection must be transferred to §65 audit proceedings
§67 cannot become a gateway for §65 type audit demands
2.28 §67(10) Application — Commissioner as Magistrate
TAXPAYER SAFEGUARD
§67(10) of CGST Act makes §165(5) of Cr.PC applicable to these proceedings
Word 'Magistrate' substituted with 'Commissioner'
Taxpayer can file APPLICATION to Commissioner before replying to SCN
📝 Under Cr.PC §165(5), a person whose premises were searched has the right to get copies of all seized documents and the search list. In GST, this right is exercisable before the Commissioner (as substitute for Magistrate). This application (Letter H in Handbook) must be filed before replying to SCN — to know exactly what evidence Revenue has collected.
💡 Before filing detailed reply to SCN after §67 proceedings: File Letter H application under §67(10) requesting Commissioner to direct production of: (i) copy of reasons recorded for authorization, (ii) copy of INS-01, (iii) copy of INS-02, (iv) copy of seized documents, (v) copy of panchanama. This builds your evidentiary record for challenge.
Ch.3 §130 — Confiscation | Nature | Five Offences | Fine-in-Lieu | Distinction from Seizure ⇧ Index
⚖️ Section 130 — Confiscation | Due Process Even for Punishment
3.2 Nature of Confiscation
Confiscation = procedure to deliver punishment in respect of offending articles
Inflicts monetary burden on offender
NOT an emergency power (unlike §67)
📝 Confiscation is punishment — not a provisional measure. This means due process is available in full. It is not rushed like an emergency seizure. The courtesies of due process — notice, hearing, speaking order — all apply to confiscation proceedings.
3.3 Seizure MUST Precede Confiscation
MANDATORY SEQUENCE
Seizure (§67) → must precede → Confiscation (§130)
No confiscation without prior seizure
If articles were not secreted → no seizure → no confiscation
📝 Confiscation cannot be the first step. If the inspection found no secreted articles (no seizure), there can be no confiscation. The absence of seizure is a complete defence against confiscation proceedings.
3.4 Five Offences Attracting Confiscation — §130(1)
Only offending articles liable — NOT all goods in premises
Five specific categories of offending conduct listed in §130(1) clauses (a) to (e)
Must match EXACTLY to one of the five — no expansion beyond the list
📝 Revenue cannot confiscate goods just because there was an evasion. The goods must be the SPECIFIC goods that were part of the offence listed in one of the five clauses of §130(1). Only "offending articles" — not all goods in premises.
3.5 Seizure vs Confiscation
Seizure: To secure and identify — does NOT transfer property
Confiscation: Transfers property of offending articles to Government
Separate proceedings for goods and conveyance (§130 + §3.10)
3.11 Option to Pay Fine-in-Lieu
TAXPAYER OPTION
Before confiscation order → taxpayer may pay 'redemption fine' in lieu of confiscation
Fine = NOT a tax; penalty = NOT a tax
Penalty is not a 'tax' (§3.13)
📝 You can "buy back" your goods from confiscation by paying a fine. This fine is NOT admission of tax liability — it is just the price of getting goods released from confiscation proceedings. Paying the fine does not prejudice your challenge to the underlying tax demand.
Ch.4 §68/§129 — Interception of Conveyance | EWB Verification | Discrepancy & Penalty | Steps ⇧ Index
🚛 Section 68 — Inspection During Movement | E-Waybill | No Reassessment Allowed
4.4 No Preconditions for Interception
DISTINCT from §67
Interception does NOT require any preconditions — no intelligence, no suspicion needed
Any authorized Proper Officer can intercept any conveyance
Cannot question REASONS for selecting specific conveyance
📝 Unlike §67 inspection (which needs "reasons to believe"), §68 interception needs NO prior intelligence. Any truck can be stopped. But the scope of interception is LIMITED — only EWB/document verification. Not a reassessment.
4.3 Limited Scope of Verification
CANNOT DO
Interception ≠ reassessment of:
• Validity of 'other than supply' movement
• Correctness of 'form' of supply
• Classification / exemption claimed
• Valuation / nature of supply
• Any other matter not relevant to §68 + Rule 138A
CAN ONLY VERIFY
• Invoice or delivery challan (if supply / if not supply)
• EWB with Part A and Part B correct and complete
Nothing outside this limited role
📝 If you are transporting goods with a valid invoice and valid EWB — the officer cannot question your GST rate, classification, or whether it is a supply or not. That is a tax controversy — not an EWB verification issue. The officer's role is strictly limited to document checking.
4.2 Authority Extinguished on Delivery
Where movement is COMPLETED without documents and conveyance not intercepted during movement → authority of inspection is EXTINGUISHED due to completion of movement
No provision for post-facto inspection after delivery
📝 If goods arrived at destination without an EWB and no officer intercepted the truck — the §68 power is dead. There is no "post-facto" inspection of movement. The movement is complete. Only §122 minimum penalty applies as a separate proceeding.
4.5 §129 Penalty Steps
Even SLIGHTEST discrepancy = §129 penalty (no discretion except Circular 64/38/2018-GST minor errors)

Step sequence:
MOV-01 (statement) → MOV-02 (inspection order) → MOV-04 (report within 3 days) → MOV-06 (detention) → MOV-07 (SCN within 7 days) → MOV-09 (penalty order within further 7 days) → MOV-05 (release) or e-auction within 15 days
📝 §129 proceedings have strict time limits at each step. If SCN is not issued within 7 days of detention, or penalty order not passed within 7 days of SCN — the detention becomes illegal. Monitor every deadline. A missed deadline = release of goods.
💡 From 1 Jan 2022: Confiscation proceedings under §130 have been delinked from §129. MOV-10 (second notice for confiscation) is no longer applicable. §129 proceedings end with penalty. Separate §130 proceedings required for confiscation (if applicable).
Ch.5 §69 — Arrest | Offences | Bail | Prosecution vs Adjudication | Consultant Protection ⇧ Index
🔒 Section 69 — Arrest | Only for §132 Offences | Strong Safeguards
📝 Arrest in GST is the most serious power. It can only be exercised for specific §132 offences — not for every case of tax demand. The safeguards are strong: bail is available, forced payments can be refunded, prosecution and adjudication are parallel (not sequential), and arrest of a CA/consultant requires showing them to be the "mind" behind the offence.
§132 Offences — Triggering Arrest
Cognizable & Non-bailable offences (≥ ₹5 crore tax evasion):
§132(1)(a) to (d) — supply without invoice, fraudulent refund, wrongful ITC, fabricated documents
Other offences (≥ ₹2 crore) = bailable
All offences below ₹1 crore = no arrest
📝 Arrest is reserved for the most serious cases (≥₹5 crore, fraud). For smaller evasions (₹2-5 crore range), arrest is possible but offences are bailable. Below ₹1 crore — no arrest at all. The tax amount in dispute (not just the demand) determines the category.
5.13 Prosecution vs Adjudication — Parallel Proceedings
Radheshyam Kejriwal v. State of WB (Cr.Apl.1097/2003) cited in CBIC Instruction 4/2022-23 [GST-Inv.] dated 1 Sept 2022:
(i) Adjudication and criminal proceedings may be launched simultaneously
(ii) Decision in adjudication NOT necessary before prosecution
(iii) Both proceedings are independent in nature
📝 Revenue can arrest you AND issue SCN at the same time. Getting an SCN answered does not stop prosecution. But: if adjudication result shows NO violation on MERITS — then prosecution on same facts cannot continue (different from exoneration on technical grounds).
KEY PROTECTION
If exoneration in adjudication on MERITS (allegation found not sustainable at all) → criminal proceeding on same facts cannot continue
But technical exoneration does NOT stop prosecution
5.12 Anticipatory Bail
AVAILABLE EVEN FOR
Non-bailable cases (≥₹5 crore) → Anticipatory bail under §438 Cr.PC available
May be sought from Magistrate or directly from High Court
NOT barred even when offence is 'cognizable and non-bailable'
📝 Even in the most serious GST cases (>₹5 crore, fraud), anticipatory bail is available. File before Magistrate first; if refused, go to High Court. Important conditions typically imposed: surrender passport, report regularly to officer, don't tamper with evidence.
Remand:
First custody = Police remand (max 15 days) presented each time to Magistrate
Judicial remand beyond 15 days by Magistrate application → max 60 days
5.14 Non-Voluntary DRC-03 — Refund
Any non-voluntary payment of GST through Form GST DRC-03 to avoid arrest → IMMEDIATELY claim as refund under 'others' category under §54
Refund does NOT reference underlying investigation proceedings
Amit Gupta v. DGGI [2022] 141 taxmann.com 209 (Delhi): Tax in default can be paid through CREDIT LEDGER also — useful for defending bail petitions
📝 If forced to pay during arrest threat — immediately file refund application. The Proper Officer must issue acknowledgement under Rule 90 (per Circular 125/44/2019-GST). The refund process is completely separate from the investigation. You get your money back while fighting the case.
Ch.6 §70 — Summons | Scope | Cannot Collect Intelligence | Taxpayer's Response Rights ⇧ Index
📜 Section 70 — Summons | Judicial Process, Not Administrative Convenience
📝 Summons under §70 is NOT a routine administrative letter. It is a quasi-judicial process with the backing of criminal law for non-compliance. But it also has strict limits — it cannot be used to build a case from nothing, collect intelligence, or harass taxpayers into admission.
Scope of §70
Proper Officer may summon any person to:
(i) Give EVIDENCE
(ii) Produce DOCUMENTS
In any inquiry being made by him
📝 Summons requires an "inquiry" to already be underway. You cannot be summoned for a pre-inquiry fishing expedition. The purpose must be to gather evidence for a specific inquiry — not to generate evidence to start an inquiry.
Cannot Collect Intelligence via Summons
Intelligence cannot be collected from taxpayer by issuing summons or non-descript letters
Summons to collect intelligence = misuse of §70
Taxpayer within rights to object to validity of such summons
📝 If Revenue is issuing summons to ask "do you know person X?" or "did you supply to company Y?" without any ongoing inquiry into your affairs — that is not a valid summons. That is intelligence gathering disguised as legal process. You may object politely but firmly.
Taxpayer Response to Summons
MUST DO
Respond to valid summons
Produce documents when required
Give truthful answers to questions
NEED NOT DO
Admit liability or sign confessional statements
Accept characterization of transactions by officer
Accept legal conclusions as facts
📝 Produce the documents. Answer factual questions truthfully. But you do not have to accept the officer's legal interpretation of your transactions. "I supplied goods — but whether it is a taxable supply is a legal question on which I reserve my right to contest" is a perfectly valid position.
💡 Letter G in Handbook Appendix: "Confirmation of Final Position" — formally communicates that no director/employee is authorized to accept liability, all authorizations except for factual information are rescinded, and further proceedings as per law may be pursued. This cuts off the informal pressure while remaining cooperative.
⚔️ Hearing Arsenal — Inspection, Search, Seizure & Arrest under GST ⇧ Index
These ratios are for use in personal hearings. State them confidently and cite the case name.
Nazir Ahmed v. King Emperor | AIR 1936 PC 253
Privy Council | 1936 | Foundational Rule — Applicable throughout all §67, §68, §69, §70 proceedings
Key Ratio
Where a power is given to do a certain thing in a certain way, the thing must be done in that way or not at all. Tainted discovery cannot be acted upon. Illegality by way of 'lack of jurisdiction' is so profound that not even section 160(2) of CGST Act can come to the rescue of the demand consequent to such tainted discovery.
When to Use — The very first weapon in every inspection/search/seizure reply. When authorization was not in proper form, when reasons to believe were not recorded, when the scope of inspection exceeded authorization, when follow-up correspondence was issued without authority — cite this to establish the taint and the consequence: entire proceeding is void ab initio.
EP Royappa v. State of TN | AIR 1974 SC 555
Supreme Court of India | 1974 | Chapter 1 | Due Process Cannot Be Bypassed
Key Ratio
Passion to protect interests of Revenue does not authorize bypassing the 'due process' laid down in the law.
When to Use — When adjudicating/investigating officer justifies procedural violations by saying "we are protecting revenue interest." Counter: Revenue interest does not override due process. The procedure prescribed in law is itself the protection Parliament provided to the State — shortcutting it is illegal, not protective.
CCE v. Brindavan Beverages (P) Ltd. | (2007) 213 ELT 487 (SC)
Supreme Court of India | 2007 | Chapter 1 | Defective Notice = Fatal to Demand
Key Ratio
A defective notice is incurable and fatal to the demand. Section 75(7) of the CGST Act states that the grounds on which an order may be passed must be the very grounds on which the notice was issued. No adjudicating authority is empowered to cure deficiencies in the notice.
When to Use — (i) When SCN is vague (no specific allegation, no evidence annexed). (ii) When adjudicating order goes beyond the grounds of SCN. (iii) When SCN mentions §73 but order imposes §74 penalties (fraud not alleged in notice). Cite: the order falls with the notice — defective notice = defective order = fatal demand.
State of Orissa v. Dr (Ms.) Binapani Dey | AIR 1967 SC 1269
Supreme Court of India | 1967 | Chapter 1 | Natural Justice Mandatory Even Without Statute
Key Ratio
Even where a statute does not require specifically that principles of natural justice are to be followed, they must be followed because when any proceeding is capable of adversely affecting the rights of any person, adherence to the principles of natural justice is imperative. Absence of specific requirement does not authorise injustice in the very process of adjudication. A notice cannot expect the noticee to 'fill in the blanks' — notice must put noticee at notice in a clear, complete, and comprehensive manner about the allegations along with evidence in support.
When to Use — (i) When SCN lacks specificity or evidence. (ii) When personal hearing was not offered. (iii) When investigation officer adjudicates (violation of Nemo Judex). (iv) When reply was not considered. Cite to establish that natural justice was violated even without needing to find a specific statutory mandate for it.
Chuharmal v. UoI | AIR 1988 SC 1384
Supreme Court of India | 1988 | Chapter 1 | Evidence Act Standards Apply to Taxation
Key Ratio
Since equitable principles of common law are contained in Indian Evidence Act, their applicability to taxation matters is not barred and must be admitted to establish facts for the purpose of determination of question of law which relies on established facts. It is not sufficient to merely doubt the correctness of self-assessment and leave the taxpayer to prove otherwise — statements must be made responsibly.
When to Use — When Department makes allegations without substantive evidence — "we observed, we believe, we suspect." Counter: the standards of Indian Evidence Act must be met before the taxpayer is required to rebut. Suspicion ≠ evidence. Doubt ≠ proof of evasion.
Self-Assessment Regime — §59 CGST Act | Burden of Proof in GST
CGST Act Section 59 | Chapter 1 | Applicable in all demand/SCN proceedings
Key Principle
GST is a self-assessment based tax regime. Revenue must show that a prima facie case exists against the taxpayer's determination of liability. It does not suffice to put forward an alternate interpretation and leave the taxpayer to prove the correctness of the self-assessment carried out. After all, in a self-assessment based tax regime, the Revenue must show that a prima facie case exists — to do otherwise is violative of the mandate in section 59 of the CGST Act. Narayan Bhagwantrao Gosavi Balajiwale v. Gopal Vinayak Gosavi AIR 1960 SC 100: when both sides introduce evidence, the question of burden of proof becomes academic.
When to Use — Opening argument in every personal hearing: "This is a self-assessment regime. My client has self-assessed liability correctly. Revenue must demonstrate a prima facie case to the contrary. An alternate interpretation without evidence is not enough to override my client's self-assessment under §59 CGST Act." This shifts the burden back to Revenue before you even address the merits.
'Secreted' Article Doctrine — §67(2) Seizure | Only for Secreted Articles
CGST Act Section 67(2) | Chapter 2 | Applicable in all search/seizure challenges
Key Principle
Seizure under section 67(2) is legally permissible only if the articles 'are secreted.' In other words, unless articles 'are secreted,' they cannot be seized under section 67. 'Secreted' does not merely refer to articles lying around but refers to those coupled with the use of some 'device' that makes their detection impossible. Where goods are capital goods and inventory that are not liable to confiscation under any clause of section 130(1), they cannot be subject to seizure. Seizure is not a form of 'recovery' and cannot be used as a mechanism to recover taxes. Cash is not seizable as 'goods' under section 67(2) since cash is not goods liable to confiscation under any of the five clauses of section 130(1).
When to Use — (i) When goods are seized that are properly recorded in books: argue goods are NOT secreted — no device of concealment was employed — seizure is without jurisdiction. (ii) When cash is seized during inspection: argue cash cannot be seized as goods under §67(2). (iii) When goods at unregistered additional place (openly displayed, GSTIN visible): argue no "device" of concealment — not secreted.
Authorization — Exhausted by Exercise | No Sub-delegation | No Follow-up
CGST Act Section 67 | Chapter 2 | Applicable in challenges to extended/repeated inspections
Key Principle
Authorization in Form GST INS-01 stands 'exhausted by exercise' — it is specific to the specific officer (no sub-delegation allowed), at specified places (precise and exhaustive, not vague and inclusive), within specified time (day and date stated). Once authorization has been executed and the Authorized Officer exits the premises, the authorization expires. One authorization cannot be used for multiple visits to premises. Once authorization is exhausted and officers exit, there is no provision in law to issue 'endorsement' or 'letters' calling for explanation by way of 'follow-up correspondence.' Intelligence cannot be collected from taxpayer by issuing summons or non-descript letters after conclusion of section 67 proceedings.
When to Use — (i) When officers come back for a second visit on the same INS-01: challenge — authorization exhausted on first visit. (ii) When follow-up letters seek explanation of specific transactions: challenge — no legal basis for such letters after §67 is concluded; not obliged to respond. (iii) When sub-officer conducts inspection instead of named Authorized Officer: challenge — no sub-delegation allowed.
Vigilantibus Non Dormientibus Jura Subveniunt | Demand Justice First
Latin Maxim | Chapter 1 | Strategy for Approaching High Court
Key Principle
The law will not help those who sleep on their rights. If a taxpayer wishes that rights, remedies and safeguards provided by Legislature be followed, then its abuse must be actively contested not by ignoring it but by politely 'putting at notice' the Proper Officer. Only after the Proper Officer is put at notice that the proposed action is not in accordance with the mandate in law and in spite of this notice the Proper Officer proceeds, then the High Court will entertain a complaint or petition to interfere. Merely on apprehension that justice will not be done, one cannot rush to High Court.
When to Use — When preparing to file a Writ Petition against an illegal §67 inspection or §70 summons: ensure first that you have written a polite formal letter to the Proper Officer pointing out the illegality. This letter becomes the basis for the High Court to entertain your petition — showing you tried to "demand justice" first before approaching the court. Also useful when Department argues "you participated in proceedings without objection."
Radheshyam Kejriwal v. State of WB | Prosecution vs Adjudication | CBIC Instruction 4/2022-23
Supreme Court | Criminal Appeal 1097/2003 | Chapter 5 | Arrest/Prosecution
Key Ratio
Adjudication proceedings and criminal proceedings can be launched simultaneously. Decision in adjudication proceedings is not necessary before initiating criminal prosecution. Both proceedings are independent. Findings against person in adjudication are not binding on criminal prosecution. BUT: if exoneration in adjudication is on merits — where allegation found not sustainable at all and person held innocent — criminal proceedings on same set of facts and circumstances cannot be allowed to continue, the underlying principle being the higher standard of proof in criminal cases. Trial of person shall be in abuse of process of court.
When to Use — (i) When defending against arrest in cases where adjudication proceedings are already underway: argue parallel proceedings but use merit-based exoneration as shield if adjudication concluded favourably. (ii) When prosecution continues after SCN dropped on merits: cite — continuation of prosecution = abuse of process of court. (iii) Important: technical exoneration (limitation, procedural) does NOT stop prosecution — only merit-based exoneration works.
